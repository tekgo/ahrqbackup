Upper gastrointestinal (GI) cancer: proportion of patients with oesophageal or gastric cancer who are referred to a dietitian within 4 weeks of diagnosis.

Brief Abstract

Description

This measure is used to assess the proportion of patients with oesophageal or gastric cancer who are referred to a dietitian within 4 weeks of diagnosis.

Note from the National Quality Measures Clearinghouse: This measure is part of the Cancer Quality Performance Indicators (QPIs) collection. For more information, including a complete list of QPI measure sets, please visit the Healthcare Improvement Scotland Web site.

Rationale

All patients with oesophageal or gastric cancer should be screened using a validated nutritional screening tool to assess nutritional risk. Those at risk of nutritional problems should have access to a state registered dietitian to provide appropriate advice (Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network [SIGN], 2006).

Poor nutritional status is a risk factor for poor tolerance of treatment whether curative or palliative and can impact greatly on quality of life (Bozetti et al., 1998).

Patients who are suitable for radical treatment, e.g., surgery, and who are malnourished benefit from nutrition support prior to treatment and all patients who have surgery benefit from early post-operative enteral feeding. Both can reduce complications such as sepsis, poor wound healing and reduce length of stay (Arends et al., 2006).

Prescriptive Standard

Target: 85%

The tolerance within this target accounts for situations where referral to dietetics is not appropriate, for example patients who are asymptomatic at diagnosis, e.g., some early cancers where there is no weight loss, normal swallowing mechanism, those where nutrition support would be futile, e.g., end of life situations, patient refusal.
